A TRAC Lease — Terminal Rental Adjustment Clause lease — is the most popular commercial vehicle lease structure in the United States. It gives commercial operators the lower monthly payments of a lease while preserving the option to purchase the vehicle at the end of the term. Unlike a consumer or closed-end lease, a TRAC lease is purpose-built for business use and gives the lessee meaningful financial flexibility at term end that consumer leases do not offer.

The Core Mechanics

At the start of a TRAC lease, the lender and lessee agree on a residual value — the projected fair market value of the truck at lease end. Monthly payments are calculated on the difference between the vehicle's purchase price and that residual, plus interest. Because you are only financing the depreciation portion rather than the full purchase price, monthly payments are meaningfully lower than a comparable loan on the same vehicle. A $70,000 F-450 on a 48-month TRAC lease with a $28,000 residual means payments based on $42,000 of depreciation, not $70,000 of purchase price. That difference translates directly to lower monthly cash outflow.

The Terminal Rental Adjustment Clause

The defining feature of a TRAC lease is the clause itself. At the end of the lease term, the lessee has three options: purchase the truck at the agreed residual value, return the vehicle, or refinance the residual into a new loan. If the actual market value of the truck at term end exceeds the contractual residual — which often happens with well-maintained commercial trucks that hold value — the lessee captures that upside. If the truck is worth less than the residual, the lessee may owe the difference upon return, which is why maintaining commercial vehicles properly throughout the lease term matters financially.

Tax Treatment

TRAC lease payments are generally treated as operating expenses for tax purposes, meaning the full monthly payment may be deductible as a business expense in the year paid. This differs from a loan, where only the interest portion and Section 179 deduction provide tax benefit. The appropriate tax treatment depends on how the lease is structured and your business's accounting method — consult your CPA to determine which structure produces the better after-tax outcome for your situation. For high-income business years, a TRAC lease's fully deductible payments can be particularly advantageous compared to the year-one spike of a Section 179 deduction.

Mileage and Maintenance Considerations

Unlike a commercial loan, TRAC leases are typically written with a contracted annual mileage limit. Exceeding that limit triggers per-mile overage charges at term end, which can be significant for high-mileage operations. For commercial truck operators running 25,000 or fewer miles per year, a TRAC lease can be structured comfortably. For oilfield service, long-haul delivery, or agricultural operations running 40,000 to 60,000 miles per year, a commercial loan is almost always the better structure unless a higher mileage allowance is negotiated at lease inception. Discuss your actual annual mileage honestly with Peoria Ford's commercial team before selecting a structure.

Why Commercial Operators Choose TRAC

The TRAC lease is the financing structure of choice for fleet managers who need to preserve working capital, maintain consistent monthly cash flow, and retain flexibility at term end. Construction companies replacing trucks on a four-year cycle, HVAC companies running technician fleets, and delivery operations managing vehicle count precisely all benefit from the lower payment structure.
