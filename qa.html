Build & Price

Start here. Configure your truck before you talk to a dealer. Compare specs, options, and pricing across the entire Ford commercial lineup.

Start with the chassis that matches your payload and towing requirements. From there, configure your cab style (Regular, SuperCab, Crew Cab), engine (gas, diesel, or PowerBoost hybrid), drivetrain (4x2 or 4x4), and option packages.

Ford's Build & Price tool lets you configure any model from Transit vans to F-750 chassis cabs. FordCommercial.AI helps you match specifications to your actual work requirements—so you're not guessing on payload capacity or towing ratings.

Key specs to consider:

  • GVWR (Gross Vehicle Weight Rating) — total weight the truck can handle
  • Payload capacity — how much weight in the bed/cargo area
  • Towing capacity — conventional vs. gooseneck/5th wheel
  • Cab-to-axle length — critical for body installations

The numbers roughly correspond to payload and capability tiers:

  • F-250: Class 2 (8,501–10,000 lbs GVWR). Best balance of daily drivability and work capability. Most popular for contractors who need a truck that doubles as transportation.
  • F-350: Class 3 (10,001–14,000 lbs GVWR). Higher payload and towing. Available as SRW (single rear wheel) or DRW (dual rear wheel). DRW versions excel at heavy towing.
  • F-450: Class 4 (14,001–16,000 lbs GVWR). Chassis cab configuration. Designed for service bodies, flatbeds, and dump beds. Serious work truck territory.
  • F-550: Class 5 (16,001–19,500 lbs GVWR). Maximum Super Duty capability. Handles the heaviest service bodies, larger dump beds, and specialized equipment.

Rule of thumb: spec one class higher than you think you need. Payload capacity disappears fast once you add a service body and tools.

A chassis cab is a truck sold without a bed—just the cab, frame, and drivetrain. It's designed to accept aftermarket bodies like service bodies, flatbeds, dump beds, or specialized equipment.

Chassis cabs are available in F-350, F-450, F-550, F-600, F-650, and F-750 configurations. When you see "DRW Chassis Cab," that's a dual-rear-wheel truck ready for a body installation.

Important measurement: Cab-to-axle (CA) length determines what size body fits. Common CA lengths are 60", 84", and 108". Longer CA = longer body options.

Both have legitimate use cases. The right choice depends on your work patterns:

Choose the 7.3L Gas V8 if:

  • Lower purchase price is important
  • You drive under 20,000 miles annually
  • Cold-weather starting reliability matters
  • You want simpler maintenance (no DEF, no diesel particulate filter)

Choose the 6.7L Power Stroke Diesel if:

  • You drive 25,000+ miles annually
  • Towing heavy loads regularly
  • Maximum torque is critical for your work
  • You'll keep the truck 200,000+ miles

The diesel costs more upfront but delivers better fuel economy under load. Break-even is typically 75,000–100,000 miles depending on fuel prices.

MSRP (Manufacturer's Suggested Retail Price) is the starting point, not the final number. Commercial truck pricing includes several layers:

  • Base MSRP: The sticker price for the configured truck
  • Commercial incentives: Ford offers fleet and commercial rebates not available to retail buyers
  • Volume pricing: Dealers have discretion on markup, especially for fleet purchases
  • Upfit costs: Added separately—can range from $5,000 to $150,000+

A commercial buyer typically pays below MSRP on the truck itself, then adds upfit costs. Total out-the-door price for a work-ready truck is often 20–50% higher than the base chassis MSRP.

Factory orders typically run 8–16 weeks from order to delivery, depending on model and configuration. Add another 2–6 weeks for upfitting.

Timeline breakdown:

  • Order placement: 1–2 weeks (spec finalization, credit approval)
  • Production scheduling: 2–4 weeks (Ford queues your build)
  • Manufacturing: 2–3 weeks
  • Transit to dealer or upfitter: 1–3 weeks
  • Upfit installation: 2–6 weeks depending on complexity

Total: 3–6 months from decision to deployment. This is why many buyers choose in-stock upfitted trucks or ship-thru programs where the truck goes straight from factory to upfitter.

Upfitted Trucks — Ready to Work

Skip the wait. These trucks come with service bodies, shelving, dump beds, or specialized equipment pre-installed. Deploy immediately.

An upfitted truck has been modified from its factory configuration with aftermarket bodies, equipment, or interior systems designed for specific work applications. This transforms a stock vehicle into a purpose-built work machine.

Common upfits include:

  • Service bodies: Steel or aluminum utility bodies with compartments for tools and equipment
  • Van shelving: Interior storage systems, partition walls, ladder racks
  • Dump bodies: Hydraulic lift beds for hauling and dumping materials
  • Flatbeds: Open cargo platforms for equipment and materials
  • Specialty equipment: Cranes, bucket lifts, compressors, welders, refrigeration units

The key advantage: an upfitted truck arrives ready to generate revenue on day one. No waiting for separate installations.

Yes. There are three ways to acquire upfitted trucks:

1. In-Stock Ready-to-Work: Dealers stock pre-upfitted trucks for immediate delivery. Limited configurations but fastest deployment—drive off the lot and go to work.

2. Ship-Thru Program: Ford ships chassis cabs directly from the factory to certified upfitters. The truck arrives at the dealer fully upfitted. Most popular option for commercial buyers—factory fresh with professional installation.

3. Dealer-Arranged Upfit: Buy the chassis, dealer coordinates with local upfitter. More flexibility on configuration but adds time.

FordCommercial.AI connects you with upfitted inventory and ship-thru programs. We help you find ready-to-work trucks that match your exact specifications.

Upfit costs vary dramatically based on complexity:

  • Basic packages ($5,000–$10,000): Bed liner, toolbox, basic shelving, lighting upgrades
  • Trade packages ($10,000–$25,000): Full van shelving, partition walls, ladder racks, power systems
  • Service bodies ($25,000–$75,000): Steel/aluminum utility bodies, integrated storage, power inverters, compressor systems
  • Specialty builds ($75,000–$250,000+): Crane trucks, bucket trucks, refrigeration units, custom engineering

The ROI math: A properly upfitted truck adds 1–2 jobs per day through better organization and capability. That's $50,000–$150,000+ in additional annual revenue potential. The upfit pays for itself multiple times over.

Buying upfitted delivers several significant advantages:

Financial benefits:

  • Finance truck + upfit together in one loan
  • Single monthly payment vs. separate financing
  • Preserve cash flow for operations
  • Potential Section 179 deduction on complete vehicle

Operational benefits:

  • Deploy immediately—no waiting for installation
  • No coordination between dealer and upfitter
  • Professional installation with warranty protection
  • Truck generates revenue from day one

Upfitting later means: cash out of pocket for the upfit, 2–6 weeks with a truck sitting unusable, and two separate transactions to manage.

Ford has made it easier to locate upfitted inventory:

  • Ford Pro Commercial Vehicle Center: Dedicated commercial departments at major dealers maintain upfitted inventory
  • Ship-Thru Locator: Find trucks currently in the upfit pipeline
  • FordCommercial.AI: We aggregate available upfitted trucks and connect you directly with inventory matching your specifications

When searching, filter by: body type (service body, flatbed, dump, etc.), chassis (F-350, F-450, F-550, Transit), and location for fastest delivery.

Body Types & Vocations

Match the body type to your trade. Service bodies, utility vans, dump trucks, flatbeds, refrigerated units, wreckers—every vocation has a purpose-built solution.

A service body replaces the standard truck bed with an enclosed utility body featuring multiple lockable compartments on both sides and rear. This is the workhorse configuration for field service, repair, and maintenance operations.

Service body features:

  • Steel or aluminum construction (aluminum saves 40% weight)
  • Multiple compartments with adjustable shelving
  • Fold-down work surfaces
  • Integrated lighting (interior and exterior)
  • Power options: inverters, 120V outlets, compressed air
  • Crane or hoist mounting capability

Common users: Plumbers, electricians, HVAC technicians, utility crews, fleet maintenance, mobile repair services.

The terms are often used interchangeably, but there are distinctions:

Service Body: Fully enclosed compartments on sides and rear. Designed for organized tool storage and protection. Weather-sealed. Premium option for trades requiring extensive equipment.

Utility Body: May have open or partially enclosed compartments. Sometimes features a combination of covered sides with an open top area. More basic configuration at lower price point.

Contractor Body: Hybrid design with side compartments plus open bed area. Good for carrying both tools and materials.

For most trade applications, a full service body provides the best return on investment through organization, security, and professional appearance.

Dump truck sizing depends on material type and haul volume:

  • F-350 with 9' dump (2–3 cubic yards): Landscaping, small construction, residential deliveries. Can handle most driveways and tight spaces.
  • F-450 with 11' dump (3–4 cubic yards): Mid-size commercial work. Better payload for heavier materials like gravel and concrete debris.
  • F-550 with 12' dump (4–5 cubic yards): Heavy commercial and municipal applications. Maximum payload in the Super Duty lineup.
  • F-650/F-750 (5–16 cubic yards): Medium-duty territory. Serious hauling capacity for construction and excavation.

Important: Cubic yard capacity means nothing if you exceed weight ratings. A cubic yard of topsoil weighs ~2,200 lbs. A cubic yard of gravel weighs ~2,800 lbs. Calculate payload, not just volume.

Flatbeds excel at hauling oversized, heavy, or awkward cargo that won't fit in enclosed bodies:

  • Construction: Lumber, steel, building materials, scaffolding
  • Landscaping: Trees, pallets of sod, equipment
  • Equipment transport: Small machinery, generators, compressors
  • Agriculture: Feed, fencing, hay bales
  • Manufacturing: Raw materials, finished goods
  • Towing prep: Flatbeds with wheel lifts for light recovery

Options to consider: Gooseneck hitch (for trailer towing), stake sides (for loose cargo), headache rack (cab protection), and tie-down systems.

Trade-specific van configurations maximize efficiency:

HVAC Technicians:

  • High-roof Transit for standing height
  • Ladder rack with conduit carrier
  • Refrigerant tank storage
  • Sheet metal brake mounting area
  • Partition with pass-through for long materials

Plumbers:

  • Extended-length van for pipe storage
  • PVC/copper pipe tubes (ceiling or floor mount)
  • Fittings bins with clear labeling
  • Hot water heater transport area
  • Drain cleaning equipment mounts

Electricians:

  • Wire reel storage system
  • Conduit and cable management
  • Small parts bins for fittings and devices
  • Panel board transport area
  • Testing equipment organization

Refrigerated (Reefer) Trucks:

  • Insulated box with refrigeration unit
  • Temperature range: frozen (-20°F) to cool (35°F)
  • Common users: food service, catering, floral, pharmaceuticals
  • Available on Transit, E-Series cutaway, and chassis cabs
  • Consider multi-temp units for mixed cargo

Box Trucks (Dry Freight):

  • Enclosed cargo box for weather protection
  • Walk-in capability for loading/unloading
  • Roll-up or swing doors
  • Common users: delivery, moving, route sales
  • Sizes from 10' to 26' on Ford chassis

Ford Transit

The commercial van that dominates construction, utilities, delivery, and service industries. One platform, endless configurations.

Transit offers three roof heights to match your cargo and work requirements:

  • Low Roof (83.6" exterior height): Fits in standard parking garages. Best for: delivery routes with height restrictions, passenger transport, mobile offices.
  • Medium Roof (100.8" exterior height): Stand-up capability for most people. Best for: general commercial use, trade vans, mobile workshops.
  • High Roof (110.1" exterior height): Full stand-up height for 6'+ workers. Best for: HVAC, plumbing, electrical—any trade requiring extended time working inside the van.

Interior cargo height: Low = 51.5", Medium = 72.3", High = 81.5"

Most trade vans specify high roof for comfort and safety. Workers standing upright make fewer mistakes and experience less fatigue.

Transit Cargo Van: Complete vehicle with factory cargo area. Ready for shelving, partitions, and interior upfits. This is what most buyers picture when they think "work van."

Transit Chassis Cab: Cab and frame only—no cargo body. Designed for aftermarket bodies: box trucks, service bodies, flatbeds, specialty equipment. Think of it as a blank slate for custom builds.

Transit Cutaway: Cab with partial floor behind it. Designed specifically for box truck and shuttle bus applications. Most common configuration for 12'–16' dry freight boxes.

Choose based on your final build: Interior shelving and standard van work = Cargo Van. External body installation = Chassis Cab or Cutaway.

2024–2025 Transit offers these powertrains:

  • 3.5L PFDi V6: Standard engine. 275 hp, capable and proven. Best for: standard commercial use, moderate payloads.
  • 3.5L EcoBoost V6: Twin-turbocharged. 310 hp and significantly more torque. Best for: heavy loads, towing, mountain driving, when you need extra power.
  • E-Transit (Electric): 266 hp electric motor, 126 miles range (varies by configuration). Best for: urban delivery, route-based businesses, fleets with charging infrastructure.

Transmission: All gas engines pair with a 10-speed automatic. E-Transit uses single-speed direct drive.

AWD Option: Available on cargo vans for improved traction in snow, mud, and rough job sites.

Transit payload varies by configuration. Maximum ratings:

  • Transit Cargo Van: Up to 4,650 lbs payload (low-roof, long wheelbase, heavy-duty payload package)
  • Transit Chassis Cab: Up to 5,070 lbs payload
  • Transit Cutaway: Up to 5,210 lbs payload

Key factors affecting payload:

  • Roof height (higher roof = more weight = less payload)
  • Wheelbase length
  • Engine choice
  • Packages and options
  • AWD vs. RWD

Important: Subtract your upfit weight from payload to calculate actual cargo capacity. A fully shelved van with tools might lose 500–1,000 lbs of usable payload.

They serve different markets:

Transit Connect (Compact Van):

  • Car-like driving experience
  • Fits in standard parking spaces
  • ~145 cubic feet cargo capacity
  • Payload up to 1,570 lbs
  • Best for: urban delivery, light trade work, mobile businesses with small equipment needs

Transit (Full-Size Van):

  • Full commercial capability
  • Up to 487 cubic feet cargo capacity
  • Payload up to 4,650 lbs
  • Multiple roof heights and lengths
  • Best for: serious trade work, high-volume cargo, full shelving systems

Transit Connect is being phased out. If you need compact, consider Transit in its smallest configuration or look at other options.

Major upfitter brands for Transit shelving:

  • Adrian Steel: Modular steel shelving, good value, extensive catalog
  • Ranger Design: Aluminum systems, lighter weight, premium quality
  • Weather Guard: Commercial-grade steel, heavy-duty applications
  • Sortimo: German engineering, premium organization systems
  • Knapheide: Full van conversions, commercial-grade

Components to consider:

  • Partition wall (solid, perforated, or with window)
  • Shelving units (adjustable vs. fixed)
  • Drawer systems for small parts
  • Ladder racks (drop-down for low clearance)
  • Lighting (LED strips, motion-activated)
  • Power systems (inverter, outlets, USB)

Financing & Leasing

Finance the truck and the upfit together. One payment, immediate deployment, preserved cash flow.

Yes—and you should. Ford Pro FinSimple allows you to finance the complete package in a single transaction:

  • One loan: Truck + upfit combined
  • One payment: Simplified accounting
  • Extended terms: Spread upfit costs over 60–84 months
  • Tax advantages: Potential Section 179 deduction on complete vehicle

The alternative—financing the truck separately and paying cash for the upfit—drains working capital and delays deployment. Rolling everything together keeps cash in your business.

Commercial Loan (Purchase Financing):

  • You own the truck at end of term
  • Build equity with every payment
  • No mileage restrictions
  • Modify and upfit as needed
  • Higher monthly payments
  • Section 179 eligible

Commercial Lease:

  • Lower monthly payments
  • Return or buy at end of term
  • Potential mileage limits (varies by lease type)
  • Possible off-balance-sheet treatment
  • Easier to upgrade fleet regularly

TRAC Lease (most popular for commercial): Terminal Rental Adjustment Clause—combines lower payments with potential equity if the truck is worth more than the residual at lease end.

Commercial financing evaluates your total business profile, not just personal credit:

  • Personal credit: One factor among many—not the only factor
  • Business credit: Dun & Bradstreet score, trade references
  • Time in business: 2+ years strengthens applications
  • Cash flow: Revenue and bank statements matter
  • Industry experience: Track record in your trade

Businesses with strong cash flow often qualify even with imperfect personal credit. New businesses may need stronger personal credit or additional documentation (tax returns, bank statements).

Ford Credit and alternative lenders offer programs for various credit profiles. The commercial team at Peoria Ford can assess your situation confidentially.

Section 179 allows businesses to deduct the full purchase price of qualifying equipment—including commercial vehicles—in the year of purchase rather than depreciating over time.

2024 limits:

  • Maximum deduction: $1,160,000
  • Phase-out threshold: $2,890,000 in total equipment purchases

Vehicle requirements:

  • GVWR over 6,000 lbs (F-250 and above qualify)
  • Used more than 50% for business
  • Placed in service during the tax year
  • Upfits can be included in the deductible amount

Example: Buy a $75,000 upfitted F-450 service body truck. Deduct the full $75,000 in year one instead of depreciating over 5 years. At 25% tax rate, that's $18,750 in first-year tax savings.

Consult your tax advisor for specific guidance.

Timeline for commercial credit decisions:

  • Same day: Simple deals with established businesses, strong credit profiles
  • 24 hours: Most commercial applications with complete documentation
  • 2–3 days: Complex situations, alternative lenders, additional documentation required

Speed up approval by having ready:

  • Business name and tax ID (EIN)
  • Time in business and annual revenue
  • Personal information for guarantors
  • Business tax returns (if requested)
  • Recent bank statements (if requested)

Fleet financing refers to programs designed for businesses acquiring multiple vehicles:

  • Fleet line of credit: Pre-approved credit limit—draw as needed for purchases without repeated applications
  • Volume pricing: Better rates and terms based on purchase volume
  • Master lease agreements: Standardized terms for ongoing acquisitions
  • Fleet account management: Dedicated support for vehicle lifecycle

Qualification threshold: Generally 3+ vehicles per year, though some programs start at 5 or 10 units.

Fleet programs streamline the acquisition process—one application covers multiple vehicles, consistent terms, and often priority allocation on limited-availability models.

Upfitters & Warranty

Certified upfitters, warranty protection, and quality standards. Eliminate the risk of electrical nightmares and warranty conflicts.

QVM is Ford's certification program for upfitters who meet quality and compliance standards:

  • Training: QVM companies receive Ford-specific training on electrical systems, body mounting, and warranty requirements
  • Standards: Must follow Ford's Body Builders Layout Book and upfit guidelines
  • Inspection: Ford audits QVM facilities for quality control
  • Support: Direct access to Ford engineering for complex installations

Why it matters: QVM upfitters understand how to modify Ford trucks without voiding warranties or creating electrical problems. Non-certified shops often make mistakes that cause issues years later.

Not if done correctly. Ford's warranty policy:

  • Factory warranty remains intact for properly installed upfits
  • Upfit-related issues are the responsibility of the upfitter, not Ford
  • Unrelated systems remain covered—an improperly wired accessory doesn't void your powertrain warranty

Protect yourself:

  • Use QVM-certified upfitters
  • Get written documentation of the work performed
  • Ensure electrical connections follow Ford specifications
  • Keep all upfit invoices and installation records

Ship-thru programs offer the cleanest solution—Ford tracks the upfit as part of the original build, and everything is documented properly from day one.

Ship-thru is Ford's program where new trucks go directly from the factory to an upfitter before arriving at the dealer:

The process:

  1. Order truck with ship-thru designation
  2. Ford builds the chassis at the factory
  3. Truck ships directly to certified upfitter
  4. Upfitter installs body/equipment
  5. Completed truck delivered to dealer
  6. You take delivery of a fully built, work-ready vehicle

Advantages:

  • Single point of responsibility
  • Cleaner warranty documentation
  • Often faster than dealer-arranged upfit
  • Professional installation on a factory-fresh chassis

Several ways to locate qualified upfitters:

  • Ford Pro Upfit Locator: Search Ford's database of QVM-certified companies
  • Dealer networks: Commercial dealerships maintain relationships with local upfitters
  • NTEA membership: The National Truck Equipment Association directory
  • FordCommercial.AI: We connect you with vetted upfitters in Arizona

Questions to ask any upfitter:

  • Are you Ford QVM certified?
  • What warranty do you provide on your work?
  • Can you show examples of similar builds?
  • What's your current lead time?
  • Do you offer ship-thru capabilities?

Poor upfit work creates expensive problems:

Electrical issues:

  • Improper wiring drains batteries
  • Overloaded circuits cause fires
  • Interference with vehicle computer systems
  • Accessories that work intermittently or fail

Structural problems:

  • Bodies not properly mounted—shifting, cracking
  • Frame damage from incorrect drilling
  • Suspension overloaded beyond capacity
  • Safety equipment improperly installed

Warranty conflicts:

  • Claims denied due to improper modification
  • No documentation of work performed
  • Finger-pointing between dealer and upfitter

The cost difference between a cheap upfit and a quality QVM installation is trivial compared to these headaches. Do it right the first time.

Fleet Management

Ford Pro telematics, fleet software, and enterprise solutions. Where the big contracts live.

Ford Pro is Ford's dedicated commercial and fleet division, combining vehicles with software and services:

  • Ford Pro Vehicles: Commercial-focused trucks and vans (Super Duty, Transit, E-Transit, Maverick)
  • Ford Pro Intelligence: Telematics and fleet management software
  • Ford Pro Charging: Electric vehicle charging solutions for fleets
  • Ford Pro FinSimple: Commercial financing programs
  • Ford Pro Service: Commercial-priority maintenance and repairs

Ford Pro represents Ford's strategic focus on commercial customers—recognizing that businesses have different needs than retail buyers.

Ford Pro Intelligence is Ford's connected vehicle platform providing real-time data on your fleet:

Core features:

  • GPS tracking: Real-time vehicle location
  • Driver behavior: Speed, hard braking, idling time
  • Vehicle health: Diagnostic alerts, maintenance reminders
  • Fuel management: Consumption tracking, efficiency reports
  • Geofencing: Alerts when vehicles enter/exit defined areas

Tiers available:

  • Essentials: Basic tracking and diagnostics (complimentary on eligible vehicles)
  • Premium: Full feature set with advanced analytics

Embedded hardware in 2020+ Ford vehicles means no aftermarket device installation required.

Telematics delivers measurable ROI across multiple areas:

Fuel savings (5–15% typical):

  • Identify excessive idling
  • Optimize routing
  • Coach driver behavior

Maintenance savings:

  • Predictive alerts prevent breakdowns
  • Schedule maintenance at convenient times
  • Avoid expensive roadside repairs

Insurance savings:

  • Some insurers offer discounts for telematics use
  • Driver behavior data supports safety programs

Productivity gains:

  • Dispatch closest vehicle to jobs
  • Verify job site arrival times
  • Reduce unauthorized vehicle use

For a 10-vehicle fleet averaging 25,000 miles annually, telematics savings often exceed $10,000–$20,000 per year.

The break-even point is lower than most people think:

  • 3–5 vehicles: Basic tracking starts making sense—know where your trucks are, catch unauthorized use
  • 5–10 vehicles: Driver behavior coaching and maintenance scheduling deliver clear ROI
  • 10+ vehicles: Full fleet management becomes essential—you can't manage what you don't measure
  • 25+ vehicles: Enterprise features, dedicated fleet manager role, formal programs

Ford Pro Intelligence Essentials is complimentary on eligible vehicles, so the barrier to entry is near zero. Start with basic tracking and add features as your fleet grows.

Ford Pro Intelligence advantages:

  • Embedded hardware—no installation, no device to steal
  • Deep vehicle integration—access to all Ford diagnostic data
  • OTA updates—new features without hardware swaps
  • Single-vendor support—Ford backs everything

Third-party telematics advantages:

  • Works across mixed-brand fleets
  • May offer features Ford doesn't
  • Portable if you sell vehicles
  • Multiple vendor options to compare

Recommendation: For Ford-heavy fleets, start with Ford Pro Intelligence—it's already there. Add third-party if you need cross-brand compatibility or specific features Ford doesn't offer.

Activation depends on your situation:

New vehicle purchases:

  • Ford Pro Intelligence activates at delivery
  • Commercial dealer sets up your account
  • Immediate access to tracking and diagnostics

Existing Ford fleet:

  • 2020+ vehicles have embedded hardware
  • Contact Ford Pro to activate
  • Some older vehicles may need aftermarket devices

Fleet size considerations:

  • Under 10 vehicles: Self-service enrollment online
  • 10+ vehicles: Request dedicated fleet account manager
  • Enterprise (50+): Custom programs, volume pricing, priority support

Start at fordpro.com or work through your commercial dealer's fleet department.
